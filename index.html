Most jackets still shed rain with chemistry built to outlast us.

Fluorocarbon finishes persist in the environment for decades, and regulation is now phasing them out of textiles. The outdoor industry needs replacements that actually survive washing. This project is one attempt, built and measured in the open.

How you make cotton shed water

Nine steps, from a raindrop hitting fabric to a covalently bonded, PFAS-free coating.

A drop lands

Whether rain beads or soaks in comes down to one number: the contact angle between drop and surface. Below 90° the fabric wets out. This project's pass mark is 130° and above.

Why water beads

Two ingredients make a drop ball up: a surface water would rather not touch, and roughness that traps air beneath the drop, so it rests mostly on air instead of fibre.

The fluorocarbon era

For decades the answer was fluorocarbon side chains: CF₃ tips give the lowest surface energy known, shedding water and oil alike. But carbon-fluorine bonds barely degrade in nature. These are the forever chemicals, and regulation is phasing them out of textiles.

The PFAS-free toolbox

Silicones, hydrocarbons and waxes can match the water repellency, though not the oil repellency. Their real weakness is durability: a film that merely sits on the fibre washes away. The fix is to bond it on.

Texture, grafted on

First, roughness that cannot rinse off. Titanium dioxide nanoparticles carrying a silane coupling agent (VTMS) condense onto cotton's hydroxyl groups, grafting a permanent micro-texture onto every fibre.

The coating goes on

A waterborne, fluorine-free polymer emulsion is padded over the textured fibre and dried into an even film. On its own that film is still removable. It needs locking in place.

A crosslinker with the pin in

Enter blocked isocyanate. Each reactive NCO group is capped by a blocking agent, so the molecule stays inert in the water bath at room temperature. Heat is the trigger that takes the caps off.

130 °C releases it

In the curing oven the caps come off, regenerating reactive NCO that bonds to cotton's hydroxyls as urethane links. The tell-tale: a free-NCO infrared band at 2278 cm⁻¹, seen only after curing at 130 °C.

What survives the wash

The result is one covalent network: texture, film and fibre tied together. The real test is not the first water drop but the drop after repeated washing. That durability data is being collected now.
