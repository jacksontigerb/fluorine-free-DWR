The problem

Almost every waterproof relies on harmful PFAS.

The finish that makes rain bead off a jacket is almost always PFAS: per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, better known as the "forever chemicals." Nothing else repels water and oil quite as well, so the outdoor industry has used them for years.

The catch is that they barely break down. They build up in the environment and in us, are linked to health concerns, and are being phased out of textiles. I'm trying to match how well they work, with no fluorine at all.

The coating

How I make it.

It comes together in three parts, none of them fluorinated.

The polymer. Long carbon chains from stearyl acrylate give a surface water doesn't want to wet. A little silicone (polydimethylsiloxane) keeps the fabric soft, and an epoxy group with a blocked-isocyanate crosslinker locks it onto the cotton so it survives a wash.

The roughness. Anatase titanium dioxide nanoparticles add fine texture, aiming to lift a droplet onto a cushion of trapped air. In the end the particles moved the wetting numbers less than the binder itself did, which is its own finding.

The particle tweak. Bare anatase clumps together, so I treat it with a silane (vinyltrimethoxysilane) to help it spread evenly through the polymer and hold up better in sunlight.

I make the polymer as a waterborne emulsion, mix in the particles, and put it on cotton by dip, pad and cure.

None of these are PFAS, so they should be a slightly gentler choice than the forever chemicals. It is early though, and ongoing research will show how much better they really are.

Why the silane helps. Left to itself, the titanium dioxide coagulates into big shard-like clumps. After the silane treatment the particles are finer and sit more evenly, which is what I want spread through the coating. Both at 4000× on the SEM.

Finisterre

Testing it on real fabric.

Their fabric first, then my coating on it.

I work with Finisterre, the Cornish outdoor brand. Their waterproofs have used PFAS-free repellents since 2018, so their fabric is the right benchmark: I characterise it as supplied, then put my coating on it and compare.

In the lab

The results are in.

The programme is complete: repellency, shedding, and durability through 200 abrasion cycles and 20 washes, with the worn surfaces checked under the electron microscope. Here is what each test asked, with the numbers on the results page.

  • Wetting

    Contact angle

    How spherical a droplet sits. The coatings hold about 130°, level with the commercial finish.

  • Shedding

    Spray and roll-off

    Whether water runs straight off a tilted surface.

  • Durability

    Abrasion and wash

    The one that really counts. 200 abrasion cycles did no harm; washing is everyone's weakness.

  • Structure

    SEM-EDX, FTIR, XRF, TGA

    Checking the structure, the chemistry, and that the coating really is on the fibre.

The headline: the fluorine-free coatings bead water like the commercial benchmark and survive wear just as well. Where they still lose is shedding when fresh, washing is what wears everyone down, and contact angle alone would have missed all of it.
