The collaboration

Working with Finisterre.

My coating needs real fabric to prove itself on. That fabric comes from Finisterre, the Cornish outdoor brand. This page is who they are, why they fit this project, and what their fabric shows so far.

Who they are

A brand that already made the switch.

Finisterre started in 2003 above a surf shop in St Agnes, Cornwall, making kit for cold-water surfers. They have been a certified B Corp since 2018.

More to the point for this project: their waterproofs have used PFAS-free water repellents since 2018, well ahead of the restrictions now working through the EU. They write about that switch on their own fabric pages.

That is exactly what makes their fabric the right test bed. Their finish is a commercial, fluorine-free benchmark that already survives real use. If my coating is going to matter, it has to stand next to that.

The approach

Their fabric first, then my coating on it.

I start by characterising their fabric as supplied, including how their current DWR finish behaves, so I have a proper baseline. Then I put my coating on the same fabric and compare against an untreated control, so any change is genuinely down to what I have made.

Finisterre's own fabric, before and after their DWR finish. This is the baseline I measure against.

So far

What their fabric shows.

Their finish beads well. A droplet on their DWR fabric sits at about 132 degrees, right alongside what my coatings reach on cotton.

It sheds even better. On roll-off, the test where a tilted drop has to actually leave, their finish is the best I have measured, around 24 degrees after a wash. My coatings bead just as well but do not shed as cleanly yet.

My emulsion works on their fabric too. The emulsion-only coating reaches about 107 degrees on their fabric, lower than on cotton. Untreated, their base fabric soaks water straight in.
